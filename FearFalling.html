VR and Fear of Falling

Fear of falling: efficacy of virtual reality associated with serious games in elderly people

Authors: Levy, F., Leboucher, P., Rautureau, G., Komano, O., Millet, B. & Jouvent, R. (2016)

Fear of falling, an intense fear that affects standing or walking, often leading to individuals avoiding activities. This fear is prevalent, affecting 24% to 55% of elderly individuals, with consequences such as physical injuries, reduced quality of life and social isolation. Prior interventions had limited effectiveness, therfore this study investigates the effectiveness of VR exposure therapy adressing the both psychological and physical aspects of fear of falling.
SHORT REVIEW

General Description
+
This study investigates fear of falling, an intense fear that affects standing or walking, often leading individuals to avoid activities they are still capable of performing. Despite no apparent neurological issues, this fear is prevalent, affecting many elderly individuals, with higher rates among those who have experienced falls.
Current interventions, primarily focusing on balance, have limited effectiveness. Hence, this study proposes a novel approach considering fear of falling as an anxiety disorder. It suggests employing cognitive behavioral therapy, a proven treatment for anxiety disorders, alongside innovative techniques such as virtual reality exposure therapy (VRET) and serious games.
VR exposure, creating 3D immersive environments, and serious games, incorporating interactive technologies like Nintendo Wii, aim to address both psychological and physical aspects of fear of falling. Combining these techniques seeks to reinstate confidence in patients' physical abilities while addressing the underlying psychological fears. This study aims to assess the effectiveness of this combined approach in treating fear of falling, potentially offering a new therapeutic strategy for this prevalent and debilitating condition.
What was done?
+
16 participants took part in this study, 9 in the treatment group (Age M = 72.4 years, SD = 12.25) and 7 on the waiting list (Age M = 68.65 years, SD = 19.05). All participants were part of the pre- and post-assessment consisting of a non structured clinical assessment featuring the BDI (21-item version), Spielberger Stateâ€“Trait Anxiety Inventory (state anxiety: STAI form Y-A; trait anxiety: STAI form Y-B), Sheehan Disability Scale (SDS), and fear of falling measure (FFM). The treatment group underwent 12 weeks of once a week VR exposure therapy. Each session of 40 minutes started with a clinical interview, followed by 15 minutes of VR training in which participants had to navigate through 3D virtual worlds via VR goggles and mouse. There were different 3D virtual worlds that had increasing difficulty levels and different obstacles. For instance, city, castle, island, underground, sidewalks, narrow ways, high steps, uneven grounds. After that they played two serious games for 15 minutes. In the first one, participants should wash windows covered in foam, in the second one, participants had to defeat enemies via hand movements to the left and to the right. In the meanwhile participants on the waiting list had no change in treatment.
What are the results?
+
The groups were comparable in terms of gender distribution and age. All participants exhibited fear of falling due to traumatic origins following falls and had walking difficulties not explained by their physical condition. There were no significant differences between the groups in fear of falling severity (FFM: treatment group 9.0 (7.71),control group 34.43 (9.32), p=.351) or Sheehan Disability Scale (SDS) scores for social (treatment group 6.44 (3.17), control group 6.00 (1.15), (p=.485)) and family life (5.44 (4.07) and 4.71 (3.40), p=.709) at baseline.
Again, no differences were found in STAI-Y-A (treatment group 39.44 (14.64), control group 35.29 (8.18), p=.873), STAI-Y-B (treatment group 47.5 (9.29), control group 42.57 (7.04), p=.164), and BDI (treatment group 12.88 (SD =7.00), control group 8.29 (5.82), p=.266) scores. None of the participants had severe depression. Participants presented with various comorbidities, but these did not explain their walking difficulties.
The therapy group showed a significantly greater decrease in fear of falling post-intervention compared to the control group (FFM: treatment group -2.78 (4.82), control group 4.14 (4.3), p=.007). The treatment group also exhibited a significant decrease in state anxiety compared to the control group (STAI-Y-A: treatment group -8.86 (14.46), control group 9.8 (4.66), p=.005), but no significant difference was found in trait anxiety (STAI-Y-A: treatment group -5.50 (9.18), control group 1.60 (9.76), p=0.247) and depression scores (BDI: treatment group -3.00 (10.79), control group 3.25 (2.75), p=0.476). Changes in SDS scores for social (treatment group -1.22 (2.16), control group 0.57 (2.30), p=0.180) and family life (treatment group -1.56 (-3.78) control group 1.29 (3.77), p=0.120) impairment were not significantly different between the groups post-intervention.
Conclusion
+
This study examined the effectiveness of combining virtual reality therapy with serious games for treating fear of falling. Participants who completed 12 sessions of this combined therapy showed significantly lower fear of falling scores compared to those on the waiting list. This novel approach was innovative for two main reasons: it combined virtual reality exposure therapy with serious games for the first time, and it focused on addressing the psychological dimension of fear of falling rather than solely the balance aspect. The therapy targeted the phobic reaction through virtual exposure, considering fear of falling as an anxiety disorder. The reduction in fear of falling was specific to the intervention. The study also highlighted the potential of utilizing new technologies effectively in older populations.
Relevance
+
This study represents a unique approach by combining two distinct processes. By integrating virtual reality (VR), which engages cognitive processes and provides real-time feedback, with serious games, which promote embodiment and complement VR, the study harnesses the power of both top-down and bottom-up mechanisms. This innovative combination opens up new avenues for therapeutic interventions, demonstrating the versatility of VR technology in addressing diverse societal challenges. Moreover, the study's focus on fear of falling, a prevalent issue among the elderly, sheds light on the potential of VR to tackle age-related concerns. Traditionally, VR might not be the first technology that comes to mind when considering solutions for elderly populations. However, this study challenges such preconceptions, highlighting VR's adaptability and efficacy in catering to the needs of older individuals. Furthermore, the study's emphasis on the psychological component of fear of falling sets it apart from traditional treatments that predominantly target the physical aspects of the condition. By recognizing fear of falling as an anxiety disorder and employing exposure therapy through VR, the study addresses the root cause of the problem, paving the way for more comprehensive and effective interventions.

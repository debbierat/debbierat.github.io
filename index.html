Virtual Reality Exposure Therapy for Anxiety and Related Disorders

A metaanalysis of randomized controlled trials

Authors: Carl, E., Stein, A.T., Levihn-Coon, A., Pogue, J.R., Rothbaum, B., Emmelkamp, P., Asmundson, G.J.G., Carlbring, P.,& Powers, M.B. (2019)

Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent class of mental disorders. Exposure therapy is an effective way to treat anxiety disorders, but only 1/3 of those in need of treatment actually receive it. The use of virtual reality (VR) might be an option for closing this treatment gap, as it offers many advantages. The meta-analysis of Carl et al. (2019) investigated if VR exposure therapy is effective in itself and in comparison to conventional exposure therapy.
OVERVIEW

Focus of Study
Efficacy of Virtual Reality Exposure Therapy (VRET) versus control conditions or in vivo exposure.
Target Group
Mostly persons diagnosed with anxiety disorders: PTSD, SAD, specific phobias, panic disorder with and without agoraphobia and performance anxiety. Persons without diagnose met criteria of anxiety disorders during evaluation.
Stakeholders
Psychotherapists, VR game/Video developers, persons with anxiety or related disorders.
Average Effect-size
Overall large effect size for VRET vs control conditions (g = 0.88).
No difference between VRET and in vivo-exposure.
Other findings
Medium to large effects for VRET vs. psychological control conditions
(g = 0.78).
Large effects for VRET vs waitlist control condition (g = 0.90).
Relatively stable effects sizes over all anxiety related disorders tested.

Introduction
+

Anxiety-related disorders are the most prevalent class of mental disorders and therefore in need of research to fight this widespread suffrage of people. Cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT) in combination with exposure therapy is indicated as one of the treatment options for many anxiety-related disorders and shows to be quite effective for example obsessive-compulsive disorder, social anxiety, posttraumatic stress disorder and more. Exposure therapy describes the confrontation with anxiety-inducing stimuli in hopes of desensitizing against the specific form of anxiety and therefore reducing it. Unfortunately, only maximally a third of anxiety-related patients that could be helped with exposure therapy actually received this treatment. This is where the use of Virtual Reality might come in handy to close this treatment gap.

Virtual Reality (VR) is a technology that enables immersive experiences of visual and sometimes even auditive recordings in a three-dimensional environment. To ensure such an experience, users wear goggles that make it possible to perceive a 360-degree visual and also headphones if audio is available.

Due to VRs immersive and more reality-like character compared to watching a video on a normal screen, it lends itself not only to entertainment-based games and videos but also practical uses, e.g. in a clinical setting. For example, studies on VR use for alcohol dependency, specific phobias, social anxiety and other anxiety disorders have been conducted. VR can help exposure therapy (VRET) be in a safer and fully controlled setting compared to in vivo exposure while still being much more life-like compared to exposure via 2D videos, pictures or in-sensu exposure. For example, exposure therapy for social anxiety like shame attack exercises, in which you practice slightly embarrassing exercises to desensitize against social anxiety, have a higher chance of people reacting in an unwanted way than a VR video or environment. Moreover, studies showed that there is a substantial percentage of patients who would prefer VRET over in-vivo exposure, and it also would be easier to use on a large scale. Lastly, incentives for this study were that previous studies indicated that it is similarly effective as in-vivo exposure.
What is this study about?
+

This meta-analysis aimed to investigate the efficacy of VRET in the treatment of a broad spectrum of anxiety-related disorders compared to in-vivo exposure, CBT, waitlist and other control conditions. The authors hypothesized to observe a large effect size for VRET versus control conditions and a non-significant difference between VRET and in-vivo exposure meaning it would be a similarly effective treatment as the traditional in vivo exposure.

For this meta-analysis, they searched for controlled trials with random or matched participant assignment via databases like PsychInfo, MEDLINE and Cochrane Central Register of controlled trials, as well as by scanning reference sections with the help of a citation search tool. And lastly inquired researchers in the field for additional trials.

Inclusion criteria included studies with at least one VRET condition, random or matched condition assignment and either active or inactive control group that did not use VR. Exclusion criteria included studies with insufficient statistical data, within-subjects design, cross-over design, VR being combined with other interventions, medication or placebos and in-sensu exposure as a control.

Overall, PRISMA guidelines were followed during the selection of studies.

The meta-analysis is finally based on 30 studies with a total of 1057 participants. The studies included a wide array of control conditions as well as a wide array of anxiety-related disorders. Fourteen studies tested VRET for specific phobias, 8 for SAD or performance anxiety, 5 for PTSD, and 3 for PD. Control conditions were either in-vivo exposure, waitlist, or other psychological control conditions. Outcome measures were split up into 5 categories, namely domain-specific subjective distress, behavioural measures, cognitive ones, psychophysiological ones and general subjective distress. These measures were mostly collected via questionnaires. Most relevant to the authors’ research question was the domain-specific subjective distress. Lastly, between-group effect sizes were calculated with the help of Hedges g as to offer an unbiased effect size estimate, even though their sample size was small.

What are the results of this study?
+
VRET vs. control conditions:

Overall large mean effect size for VRET relative to waitlist and other control conditions (g=0.88, SE = 0.10, 95% CI: 0.69–1.07) and results were maintained at follow up (n=4; g=0.90, SE=0.48, 95% CI:−0.05 to 1.85). The effects for the specific control conditions, psychological control condition (g=0.78) and waitlist condition (g=.90), were also high. The results were also maintained at follow up (n=4; g=0.90, SE=0.48, 95% CI:−0.05 to 1.85).

Differences VRET vs in vivo:

Regarding the differences between in virtuo and in vivo exposure, there is a non-significant mean overall effect size (g=−0.07, SE=0.11, 95% CI: −0.28 to 0.15), in favor of in vivo conditions. At follow-up, there was a small nonsignificant effect in favor of in vivo conditions (n=7; g=-0.22, SE=0.22, 95% CI: −0.65 to 0.22). For other outcome measures, such as behavioral, cognitive outcomes and general distress the effects were also small and non-significant.

By disorder:

Sorted by disorder, there were large effect sizes for VRET regarding specific phobias (12 studies in total), social anxiety disorder and performance anxiety (7), panic disorder (2) and moderate effects for PTSD (5). There were no significant differences between VRET and in vivo exposure, and therefore only small effects in favor of in virtuo for social anxiety disorder and performance anxiety (6) and in favor of in vivo for specific phobias (5) and panic disorder (3).

Moderators:

A larger total sample size was associated with a lower study effect size in VRET versus the control conditions (β=−0.005, p=.03) but no significant effect for VRET versus in vivo exposure (p=.7). Additionally, the number of treatment sessions and the publication year had no influence on the effect size.

Quality of the meta-analysis
+
Magnitude:

The study findings reveal significant effect sizes across various anxiety-related issues, demonstrating a medium to large effect size. The effects remain stable across different anxiety-related problems.

Articulation:

The effects are presented in a differentiated manner. The effects are shown for the various control conditions and in vivo as well as for the individual areas of anxiety and related issues.

Generality:

The statements refer to many different areas of anxiety. For example, specific phobias, social anxiety disorder and performance anxiety, post-traumatic stress disorder and panic disorder. Stable medium to large effects were found across these domains. Although no studies on general anxiety disorder or OCD were included in the study, conclusions about anxiety and related disorders are valid. No statements can be made for other areas outside anxiety.

Interestingness:

The study is of great interest because it covers a wide range of anxiety disorders and related issues and thus shows that VRET is an effective and versatile tool in the field of anxiety-related disorders. Surveys have also shown that many people would prefer VRET over typical exposure therapy.

Credibility:

The meta-analysis shows stable effects across the various studies. However, there is an asymmetry in the funnel plot for VRET vs. control conditions, which could indicate a bias of publication. There is no asymmetry in the funnel plot for VRET vs. in vivo. Both plots were subjected to a trim and fill procedure that showed no change. The authors also stated that they did not consider risk of bias or the quality of the studies in their analyses.

Conclusion for research and relevant stakeholders
+
"In cases where VRET is preferable or more accessible, it can be considered an acceptable and efficacious alternative to in vivo exposure
for the treatment of anxiety-related disorders."

Advantages of VR in comparison to conventional exposure therapy:
  • control over phobogenic stimuli, allowing customization of environments to suit individual preferences
  • Greater accuracy in inducing anxiety and ability for the therapist to repeat exposure as well
  • Increased accessibility and ease of access to treatment
  • Limited unexpected events during exposure
  • Some exposures to fear can be difficult to reproduce in vivo (e.g., fear of flying, of storms) and reduction of costs (e.g., taking the plane)
  • Remaining in the clinician’s office during exposure facilitates confidentiality and enhances the sense of security by e.g. reducing the fear of judgment
  • Decreased maintenance and associated costs required for animals used for exposure
  • Reduced workload for psychotherapists by automating certain aspects of exposure therapy
Factors interfering with VRET:
  • Cybersickness could hinder treatment effectiveness or even usage
  • Lack of reactivity to the virtual environment
  • Cultural differences regarding effectiveness of treatment (e.g. countries with venomous vs. non-venomous spiders)

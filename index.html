Introduction
Anxiety-related disorders are the most prevalent class of mental disorders and therefore in need of research to fight this widespread suffrage of people. Cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT) in combination with exposure therapy is indicated as one of the treatment options for many anxiety-related disorders and shows to be quite effective for example obsessive-compulsive disorder, social anxiety, posttraumatic stress disorder and more. Exposure therapy describes the confrontation with anxiety-inducing stimuli in hopes of desensitizing against the specific form of anxiety and therefore reducing it. Unfortunately, only maximally a third of anxiety-related patients that could be helped with exposure therapy actually received this treatment. This is where the use of Virtual Reality might come in handy to close this treatment gap.

Virtual Reality (VR) is a technology that enables immersive experiences of visual and sometimes even auditive recordings in a three-dimensional environment. To ensure such an experience, users wear goggles that make it possible to perceive a 360-degree visual and also headphones if audio is available.

Due to VRs immersive and more reality-like character compared to watching a video on a normal screen, it lends itself not only to entertainment-based games and videos but also practical uses, e.g. in a clinical setting. For example, studies on VR use for alcohol dependency, specific phobias, social anxiety and other anxiety disorders have been conducted. VR can help exposure therapy (VRET) be in a safer and fully controlled setting compared to in-vivo exposure (*1) while still being much more life-like compared to exposure via 2D videos, pictures or in-sensu exposure (*2). For example, exposure therapy for social anxiety like shame attack exercises, in which you practice slightly embarrassing exercises to desensitize against social anxiety, have a higher chance of people reacting in an unwanted way than a VR video or environment. Moreover, studies showed that there is a substantial percentage of patients who would prefer VRET over in-vivo exposure, and it also would be easier to use on a large scale. Lastly, incentives for this study were that previous studies indicated that it is similarly effective as in-vivo exposure.
What is this study about?
This meta-analysis aimed to investigate the efficacy of VRET in the treatment of a broad spectrum of anxiety-related disorders compared to in-vivo exposure, CBT, waitlist and other control conditions. The authors hypothesized to observe a large effect size for VRET versus control conditions and a non-significant difference between VRET and in-vivo exposure meaning it would be a similarly effective treatment as the traditional in vivo exposure.

For this meta-analysis, they searched for controlled trials with random or matched participant assignment via databases like PsychInfo, MEDLINE and Cochrane Central Register of controlled trials, as well as by scanning reference sections with the help of a citation search tool. And lastly inquired researchers in the field for additional trials.

Inclusion criteria included studies with at least one VRET condition, random or matched condition assignment and either active or inactive control group that did not use VR.

Exclusion criteria included studies with insufficient statistical data, within-subjects design, cross-over design, VR being combined with other interventions, medication or placebos, in-sensu exposure as a control.

Overall, PRISMA (*3) guidelines were followed during the selection of studies.

The meta-analysis is finally based on 30 studies with a total of 1057 participants. The studies included a wide array of control conditions as well as a wide array of anxiety-related disorders. Fourteen studies tested VRET for specific phobias, 8 for SAD or performance anxiety, 5 for PTSD, and 3 for PD. Control conditions were either in-vivo exposure, waitlist, or other psychological control conditions (*4). Outcome measures were split up into 5 categories, namely domain-specific subjective distress, behavioural measures, cognitive ones, psychophysiological ones and general subjective distress. These measures were mostly collected via questionnaires. Most relevant to the authors’ research question was the domain-specific subjective distress though.

Lastly, between-group effect sizes were calculated with the help of Hedges g as to offer an unbiased effect size estimate, even though their sample size was small (*5).
